📖 Understanding the Data

Twilight Phases

Civil twilight ends when the Sun is 6° below the horizon — streetlights come on. Nautical twilight ends at 12° below — the horizon is no longer visible at sea. Astronomical twilight ends at 18° below — the sky is fully dark for observation. The time between astronomical twilight end (evening) and start (morning) is your true dark window.

What Affects Stargazing Quality

Moon illumination: The single biggest factor for visual astronomy. A full moon washes out all but the brightest stars; a new moon leaves skies dark enough for faint galaxies and nebulae. This tool weights moon brightness at 35% of the overall quality score when weather data is available (40% otherwise).

Cloud cover: Even a clear new moon night is wasted under overcast skies. When available, live cloud cover from the OpenWeatherMap API is factored in at 20% of the quality score and shown as a 12-hour hourly forecast. Clear skies (≤ 20% cloud cover) score highest; overcast (> 75%) scores zero.

Twilight and dark windows: True astronomical darkness begins when the Sun drops 18° below the horizon. The interval between evening and morning astronomical twilight is your dark window — the wider it is, the more time you have under genuinely dark skies. At high latitudes in summer, this window can shrink to zero.
